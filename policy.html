Redistribution of Wealth

Currently there are 15,000 millionaires (including some billionaires) living in the state of Massachusetts. Meanwhile, nearly a million live under the poverty line and millions more are still struggling to get by.

"The Commonwealth" program would levy an additional state tax of 20% on all individual revenue past the second million. This would allow:

  • Millions in grant money and the empowerment of every municipality to decide how it would best serve their people
  • Grant money to create housing cooperatives and encourage service industry cooperatives
  • Rent and housing relief
  • A $1,000 stimulus to the majority of residents in the state

There are roughly 18,000 living on the streets in Massachusetts, yet 64,000 vacation homes in Barnstable County alone. In fact, 20% of homes in Massachusetts are considered vacation homes and lay dormant for the majority of the year.

The Non-Occupancy Fee, or Playground Tax, would charge non-residents 2% of home value every month they are absent. Homeowners who were originally residents of the state and have moved elsewhere will not be subject to this tax. The generated revenue will be split between going directly to the hosting municipality (to use as they see fit) and to statewide housing relief. Vacationers may opt out of this fee by volunteering their vacant homes to be used as emergency shelter or community development.

Rent Control

All rent will be capped at its current place. If landlords are unable to keep up with expenses they may opt into the Commonwealth program and convert their units into a cooperative space. Therefore, tenants would no longer be renters but shareholders of the unit, and may sell off their share when they please.

"The Kraft Rule"

Any money gained through labor that was never, ever, directly participated in by “earners” should be considered suspect. If a billion is made from the ownership of laborers it is subject to an 80% tax. All revenue generated will be used to pay down state deficits.

What this means for Allston/Brighton:

  • Financial assistance for renters and homeowners
  • Building housing cooperatives, giving poor and young people a sense of ownership in their lives and abolishing the assumption of debt as a rite of passage
  • Grant money for schools, community centers and small businesses
  • $1,000 for each individual
  • The guarantee of housing
  • Serenity

The "Common Interest" Act

The "Common Interest" Act would ensure the legal right of workers in any noncooperative (non-employee-owned) workplace within our Commonwealth to unionize. Any — whether the business is local or a chain. No employer would be allowed to discourage collective bargaining or to put limitations on it in their contract. Once workers have agreed to form a union, they will appoint a democratic representative who will communicate on behalf of them to the state and other union operations. The business specific union may group up with other unions as they see fit. If a business is reported to have made attempts to prevent employees from unionizing, the situation will be investigated by the Department of Labor. If an outside company is aggressive to our workers, our state, or threatens legal action, the facility and materials in it will be seized by the state. Any attempt to move facilities out of the state to avoid these new laws will be viewed as aggressive.

The “Common Interest” act would also give greater deference to communities on how their natural resources are to be used or preserved. Of course, socialists take environmental protection very seriously, but Seth trusts that communities are able to govern themselves without big government. Under the “common interest” act, all regulations concerning the collection of resources (fish, trees, farm foods, etc.) will be suspended. The municipalities then will be allowed to regulate with nuance, allowing individuals to take and leave as they need. However, the state will still investigate environmental impact and, if it appears to be a threat to the environment, the municipality forfeits control.

What this means for Allston/Brighton:

  • Job security
  • Local control of parks

Rebuilding the Public Trust

Law Enforcement

We believe it is redundant (and costly) to have both local and state police. Seth will lead the charge to abolish the State Police and direct funding towards infrustruce and stop all tolls.

Transportation

Tax the endowment of all universities in Massachusetts that charged full price for screen-only learning and use the revenue to rebuild the T. Students from these universities will be granted a yearlong pass.

Political Reforms

  1. Term limits for all state offices. Service in these roles should be capped at eight years.
  2. Pay cut of $10,000 for all state elected officials This money instead will be pooled to publicly, and cheaply, finance state elections.
  3. An end to campaign fundraising for state office
  4. Introducing ranked-choice voting in the legislature
  5. Financial report of spending and revenue mailed out to every resident every year

What this means for Allston/Brighton:

  • Only eight years of me!
  • A fixed T
  • Revenge on your university if you're an indebted student
  • Better public infrastructure

Social Reforms

Sex Worker Liberation

I will propose legislation to legalize prostitution in Massachusetts.

Establish a Department of Marijuana, Alcohol, and Prostitution

This department would assume the responsibility of running all cannabis dispensaries, liquor stores and brothels. All three would become state owned, with the previous owners of the liquor stores and dispensaries well-compensated. The department would ensure that all operations are conducted safely and allow the revenue to be consolidated in one place.

Workers in this field would be state employees and entitled to a union. The state will support their union building process unconditionally but not author it. After the transition to state ownership the workers will be free to create their terms and network. Until their union is formed these workers will be connected to other unions so they may have surrogate advocacy. Revenue from these fields, in part, will be continued to be allocated to where they were previously going. Remaining revenue will be used to lower costs of community college. This legislation will not only legalize prostitution but decriminalize non-registered sex work. Meaning no sex worker will face legal reprecussions for their decision not to register, but it is highly encouraged for their own health and safety.

Prisons

Ultimately, the goal of socialists is prison abolition, but that’s not so cozy with everyone and it’s difficult to do all at once. It is unfortunate that we have no place for ex-prisoners to go after leaving incarceration and so they often end up returning. Prison structures will continue to house incarcerated souls, but as a timeout — not as a punishment.

Prisoners will be visited by social workers and aided in organizing their own interpretation of being removed from society. There will be no cell time and prisoners will be free to do what they please within the walls. The state will slowly phase out prison guards for advisors in health, cooking, literacy, and organizing. Prisoners will learn to work with each other to improve their living conditions.

The outcome will be this: a self-sufficient prison population that can secure better nourishment and personal joy with funds that are no longer being used to oppress them. There will be an LSW in place of a warden, and visiting community advisors. A few guards will remain solely to prevent harm towards the state officials. They will not patrol. Excessively violent offenders will be transferred to psychiatric facilities. Prison labor will be eliminated. Folks leaving jail will work with the state social worker/advisors to create an aftercare program. If there is no job or home for the prisoner to land after release, they may request a 30 day stay. After that, they will be put in respite or linked up to a shelter. Our push for massive prison restructuring is based in our hope for eventual prison abolition. We believe things are rigged and it forces people to make wrong choices. We believe people should still have to confront these choices, just not in a publicly funded hell.

LGBTQ Rights

We queer folk are lucky to have a home state that is safer for us than many others, with many legal protections. But there is more we can do to make it safe and “comfy” for all folks. That’s why I would propose the “Safer Home” act which would:

  • Streamline the ability to change one’s name and gender on official documents, giving the RMV the authority to advocate on behalf of residents to change all titles along with their license.
  • Massive overhaul of sex education for grades 6-12 based on an agreed-upon state curriculum devised by professionals and advocates
  • Spectrum prison assignment, ensuring that an individual is comfortable with their cell mate and has the option to be “housed” with another queer person
  • Mandatory gender education for public sectors
  • Tax credits for businesses creating single-stall gender neutral bathrooms

Adoption, Foster Care & Mentor Program

There are many great foster parents and orphanages out there, but there are also a lot of horrible ones. Every year many children are physically, sexually, and emotionally abused at these facilities. However, we are limited in our capacity for housing them if they cannot live on their own. So we must bond them with a mandatory state reporter who will work with them until adulthood or until they are in family care. Our state employees for the most part retire comfortably and are left with some spare time on their hands, or are looking for a side gig. After volunteering into this program they will be paired with a child who they will keep tabs on once a week. The child will be briefed by them on things that are appropriate and not appropriate in a foster care setting. This way the child has a trusted adult to voice concerns to, and the adult has been verified by the state through a career of public service.

Death with Dignity

Legislation to legalize assisted suicide for those dying and in tremendous pain. We believe the state has no business telling us how long our final hours will be, especially given its neglect towards us while we are truly living.

Sanctuary State

We approve of the Safe Communities Act. However, we believe a stronger proposal is necessary to protect those in our commonwealth. We prefer and will pursue legislation that will refuse entry by I.C.E. in our state.

Racial Justice Reforms

Police

As stated previously we desire to fully abolish the State Police. We can push forward racial justice and end all state tolls. At the state level we of course cannot control how municipalities conduct themselves in terms of police funding. We can however keep a close eye on stations across the state. I will propose the “Direct Justice” act, which will grant the state greater authority to intervene with regard to police misconduct. If citizens of a municipality believe there was police misconduct that was racially motivated, they may take the complaint directly to the Direct Justice Committee. The Direct Justice Committee will be a group of black legal advocates granted permission by the legislature to make a decision on the case. If they choose to accept it, the police and those who filed the complaint will present before the committee. The Committee will decide all actions to be taken and they will be legally binding.

Black Joy

I will propose legislation to create a council on Black Joy. The council will be composed of volunteer scholars, artists, and advocates to celebrate Blackness however they see fit. They will be allowed to organize the Black community through state funding however they see fit.

Environment

Environmental Justice

By granting communities the authority to use their resources as they see fit through the “Common Interest” act, this also means localities have a greater defense against polluters. If there are polluters or private industries encroaching on the public health of residents, they will be allowed by law to shut it down or otherwise seize control of it. The State cannot permit private industry to do anything without the consent of those who can be directly affected. If a City allows pollution in a manner that harms residents of a certain part of town, they are entitled to a hearing with the Direct Justice committee. In terms of immediate action, I will be there chaining myself to the building with all who want the pollution to end.

Massachusetts may be a progressive state, but its environmental habits are very poor. I will work to embody the ideas of the Green New Deal in state legislation. I will propose the “Common Green” legislation that grants every person the legal right to a “share” of the environment. This means that if there is fracking or any abuse of the environment, the money generated from it must be equally distributed to everyone in Massachusetts.

Additionally, some immediate actions:

  • Immediately ending state universities’ use of fossil fuels
  • Banning fracking
  • Funding poop-to-clean-energy projects
  • Criminal charges brought against oil companies on behalf of the State of Massachusetts

Practice Makes Perfect

The Tenants Bill of Rights

If I was interested in pushing forward with mild reforms that would never get anywhere in a conservative Democratic state house, I would still have a “D” in front of my name. I’m not. I want to meet our multiple crises head on with proposal and practice. We have many proposals concerning housing and renters rights, but we also have demands. During this campaign we will be having folks sign the Tenants Bill of Rights to signal that we are in solidarity and can flip the table over at any moment.

Here are the demands:

  1. The right to accessible housing, with no cost incurred by the renter for essential repairs.
  2. End no-fault evictions. A tenant should not lose housing for not being able to pay an increased rent.
  3. Legal council, no trial between landlord and tenant unless both parties have council.
  4. No eviction histories.
  5. No extra fees on top of first, last, and deposit.
  6. Right to reasonable renewal: a tenant should not have to commit to renewal more than two months before the lease ends.
  7. Right to knowing your landlord: information concerning your landlord’s properties and legal battles should be public record.
  8. All tenant rights publicly and easily accessible.
  9. The Right to Organize.

Fortunately, the last right is actually granted to us. We have the ability to make a renter’s union. In a district like Allston/Brighton where 80% of residents are renters, we sure do have a hell of a Union to make. Join us.

This is our community, our Commonwealth.
